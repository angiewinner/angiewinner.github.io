. Angelika Winner: PORTFOLIO

Portfolio

As a geographer, my data visualization portfolio includes many quantitative and qualitative maps across various disciplines with a heavy emphasis on geography. Please find a selection of my work listed here.


Figure 1: This map gives a geographic overview of Newark, New Jersey. It shows neighborhoods with their names, public schools, public housing developments as well as the census tracts idenitified as food deserts by the USDA. Fodd deserts are low-income neighborhoods identified as having more than 500 people living more than 0.5 miles from the nearest supermarket.


Figure 2: This figure describes Newark's historic food environment between 1911 and 1964. The map shows food retailers in 1911 which consisted of mom-and-pop wet and dry grocers, butchers, and bakers. It also shows Black-owned grocery stores from 1920. The food retail data from 1911 comes from Newark's City Directory which only listed Wgite-owned businesses. In 1920, a Black newspaper published a Black business directory of New Jersey which lists Black-owned grocers in 1920. The graph shows the dramatic reduction of food retailers in Newark over the 20th century.


Figure 3: This figure shows the Black and White population of Newark from 1890 to 2020. In 1890, Newark was a predominantly White city with large Italian, Irish, Polish, and German communities. Newark's Black population was exceedingly small and lived in the same neighborhoods as the poor White European immigrants. Only once the number of Black residents increased significantly during the Thirst and Second Great Migration, Black residents became increasingly segregated into poor and dilapidated neighborhoods in Central Newark. As suburbanization accelerated after WWII more and more White families left the city while the Black population continued to increase until the 1990s.


Figure 4: By 1940, Newark's Black population had increased significantly compared to the pre-war years. Black residents found themselves more and more segregated in the previously HOLC redlined inner city in public housing developments that were also segregated. The first housing projects to be developed in Newark were Baxter Terrace and Seth Boyden Court. The former was an integrated, majority Black development in the redlined Central Ward, while the latter was a segregated, majority White development outside the redlined inner city. All the developements built for Black residents were located in the HOLC redlined areas which were classified as blighted by the city.


Figure 5: This map shows blighted areas, public housing, as well as the various urban renewal project that changed the city significantly from the 1950s to the 1070s. While many of the urban renewal projects were located outside of blighted areas even though the funds were meant to renew blighted areas, all public housing for Newark's Black community was built in blighted areas. All public housing developments that were built outside of blighted areas were majority White.


Figure 6: This map shows results from my ethnographic work during my doctoral studies. The map shows all stores that the interviewees patronized in the Newark metro area. The size of the symbols is proportional to the number of interviewees visiting the food store. The pie chart breaks down the major food sources the interviewees reported utilizing. My work clearly shows that participants traveled far into the surounding suburbs and debunks the idea that people shop mainly in their own neighborhood.


Figure 7: Self-reported barriers to accessing healthy foods together with the patronized stores in Newark classified by average Healthy Food Retail Index score. The HFRI is based on a simplified version of the Nutrition Environment Measures Survey (NEMS) developed at the University of Pennsylvania. Food groups included in the index were 9 common fruits and 10 common vegetables, milk and eggs, whole grains, fish, lean meats, as well as frozen fruit, and vegetables. Each healthy food item received a score of one, and all scores were added up by store and avarged by store type. The color-coded table matches the barrier categories in the map and represents financial, access, transportation, individual choice, and knowledge-based barriers. Only two women I interviewed reported no barriers at all and both of them reported a much higher household income relative to the other participants classifying them as middle-class.


Figure 8: Neighborhoods ranked by total count of food retailers (left) and by average HFRI score (right). Asterisk indicated predominantly Black neighborhoods. This graphic was created in R using the ggplot2 package.


Figure 9: This figure shows results from the data scrapper for commercial real estate in Sunset Park, Brooklyn. This data model was developed to monitor economic redevelopment and potential gentrification in real-time across the neighborhood. The categories of commercial real estate for sale or rent is shown in the legend as well as in the heading above each of the map tiles. None refers to those properties where no type was asigned. The data was scrapped from LoopNet.com in June of 2024. This graphic was created in R using the ggmap and ggplot2 package.


Figure 10: This map layout shows results from a land-use analysis of Sunset Park, Brooklyn. The goal was to identify residential backyards potentially available for a backyard farming program aimed at increasing the neighborhood's food sovereignty. Backyards were calculated as lot size minus building foot print, which also includes court yards, green strips, parking lots, and walkways and not only back yards. Initial groundtruting was conducted with Google Earth to identify actual landuse of large open spaces identifed as backyards.
